Standard Cardboard Produce Box Sizes

Cardboard produce boxes are not a one-size-fits-all product. The fresh produce industry has developed a range of standardised dimensions tied to pallet compatibility, cold-chain logistics, and the biological needs of specific crops. Understanding these sizes — and the tolerances built into them — is the starting point for any procurement decision.

The most common dimension families in international trade are the Full Telescopic Design (FTD) boxes, which come in footprints of roughly 600 × 400 mm (aligned with the Euro pallet module) and 500 × 300 mm for half-module configurations. In the North American market, the dominant sizes track the GMA pallet: 48 × 40 inches at the pallet level, meaning individual box footprints cluster around 20 × 13 inches and 15 × 10 inches for half-trays. For citrus specifically, the 5 kg and 15 kg net-weight boxes remain the global standard, with external dimensions of approximately 300 × 400 × 275 mm and 300 × 500 × 200 mm respectively.

Corrugated board grade matters as much as outer dimension. Standard fresh-produce boxes typically use B-flute or C-flute single-wall corrugated board rated at 32 ECT (edge crush test) to 44 ECT. High-humidity cold stores and refrigerated containers may require double-wall C/B-flute boards, which offer substantially better stacking strength when moisture-laden air condenses on box surfaces during transit.

Stacking strength is rated through the BCT (box compression test). A standard 15 kg citrus box under dry conditions needs a BCT of at least 800 N. However, at 80–90% relative humidity — typical of a reefer container — BCT can drop by 50–70%. This is the critical engineering reality behind wax coatings and moisture-resistant variants.

Wax Coating: Benefits, Limits, and the Recycling Question

Wax-coated cardboard produce boxes have been a cold-chain staple since the 1950s. A paraffin or blended wax layer — applied via curtain-coat or cascade methods — dramatically improves moisture resistance, extending usable BCT strength in humid conditions by as much as 3–4 times compared to uncoated board.

The process typically deposits 10–35 g/m² of wax onto the outer liner and, in heavy-duty applications, onto the inner flute tips as well. Cascade-wax boxes (where wax penetrates the flute) outperform simple curtain-wax boxes in sustained ice contact, making them the standard for wax-coated berry trays, broccoli, and other iced-down products. The trade-off is cost: cascade-wax boxes are roughly 25–40% more expensive per unit than equivalent dry-strength corrugated.

Wax coating introduces a significant limitation that any responsible buyer must understand: wax-coated boxes are not recyclable via standard paper-recycling streams. The wax contaminates pulping equipment and is rejected by most paper mills. Many municipalities classify them as contaminated waste, meaning they end up in landfill even when conscientious buyers attempt to recycle them. The EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) revision currently being phased in places increasing pressure on wax-impregnated fibre-based packaging. Buyers sourcing for EU markets should verify regulatory timelines with their compliance teams.

An alternative gaining traction is clay-coated or polymer-barrier corrugated, which offers improved recyclability (some variants are commercially repulpable) while still achieving meaningful moisture resistance — though typically not at the same level as full cascade-wax boxes when exposed to direct ice contact.

Cardboard vs Plastic: When to Choose Each

The cardboard-versus-plastic debate in fresh produce packaging is often framed emotionally, but the decision should be driven by supply-chain economics, regulatory context, and product requirements.

Choose corrugated cardboard produce boxes when:

  • Your product is a single-use trip (no return logistics) and total delivered cost matters most.
  • Your buyers have composting or open-fire disposal infrastructure (cardboard degrades; plastic crates require collection logistics).
  • You need bespoke branding or variable print — corrugated takes high-quality flexo and litho printing; most plastic crates do not.
  • Your export destination enforces phytosanitary wood restrictions that indirectly favour non-wood fibre packaging.
  • Shipping volumes are irregular and you cannot justify the deposit/return system that plastic crates require.

Choose plastic reusable produce containers when:

  • You operate a closed-loop supply chain with consistent, high-volume flows between fixed origin and destination points.
  • Your product requires pressure washing between trips (seafood, high-pathogen-risk environments).
  • Long-term total cost of ownership matters more than per-trip unit cost — plastic crates used 50+ times typically cost less per use than corrugated.
  • Stack stability under automated sorting is critical, as plastic crates offer more consistent dimensional tolerances.

In practice, many large export operations run hybrid fleets: plastic reusable crates for high-volume domestic or regional lanes, and corrugated cardboard produce boxes for export lanes where return logistics are infeasible.

Export Compliance for Cardboard Produce Boxes

Corrugated cardboard is classified as wood-derived material in most phytosanitary frameworks, but processed sufficiently that it does not require ISPM 15 heat treatment — a significant advantage over solid wood crates and pallets. However, a number of compliance points still apply.

EU compliance: Boxes entering the European Union must comply with Commission Regulation (EC) 1935/2004 and related Food Contact Materials (FCM) legislation if the box is in direct contact with food. This applies particularly to wax coatings and ink formulations — buyers should request food-contact compliance declarations from their supplier. The EU PPWR (Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation) revision, expected to take effect progressively from 2030, will impose recyclability and recycled-content targets that will affect wax-coated corrugated significantly.

US import requirements: USDA and FDA both have jurisdiction over food packaging materials. Corrugated boxes must meet 21 CFR 176.170 (paper and paperboard in contact with aqueous and fatty foods) and 21 CFR 176.180 for dry food contact. Wax-coated boxes must use food-grade paraffin only.

China, Japan, South Korea: These markets have their own food contact material standards that require testing documentation. GB 4806.8 (China) and GB 9685 apply to food contact paper and board. Buyers exporting to Asian markets should obtain GB-compliant test reports from their corrugated manufacturer before the first shipment.
